The learning ladder

One idea per mission. Never two.

Every mission names the single concept it teaches, and par is what forces her to actually learn it — a solution that ignores the lesson doesn't fit in the line budget. Worlds 1–4 are fully authored with hand-verified grid traces; 5–8 are sketched.

The rules that make it teach

Why the curriculum can't be gamed.

Law 1

Parts add commands, not stats

Every unlock is a new word she can write — never a number that goes up. If a part doesn't add one new word or one new decision, it doesn't ship.

Law 2

You can't buy what you haven't learned

Coins build, but lessons unlock. There is no path around the curriculum.

Law 3

Every trap has a coded exit

Frozen, tangled, blinded, stuck — each status is readable from her program. Every hazard is also a conditionals lesson.

Law 4

Nothing dies

Bots get tagged, dented, towed and rebooted. A test greps the engine for words like "kill" and fails the build.

Law 5

Numbers arrive only after they're felt

The SPEED meter appears the moment she first feels the drag of cargo — not before. She names the sensation, then meets the number.

Law 6

The engine knows no story

Core, workbench, theme pack. The fiction never touches the simulator — so Mars is a content drop, not a sequel.

Par

Par is the teacher

Measured in editor lines. W2M2's par of 4 is unhittable without a loop — the same route unrolled is 28 lines. The lesson isn't suggested; it's the only thing that fits.

Hints

Three rungs, never the answer

Nudge → concrete sub-goal → worked example of the first leg only. She always writes the ending herself.

Free

Errors cost nothing

A soft, confused boop from the bot. Never a buzzer, never a red X, never a point. Trying is always free.

The two open questions on this page. Worlds 5–8 are sketched, not authored — they need the same hand-verified grid treatment as 1–4, and that's the largest remaining content job. And the 24 authored missions become the golden test suite: each one already specifies the arena, the author's exact code, and the expected outcome, so each is an integration test that writes itself. Nothing ships until they all pass.
