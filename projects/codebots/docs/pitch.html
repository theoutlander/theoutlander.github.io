The idea

The joy isn't finishing the lesson. It's watching your machine obey you.

Everyone who has ever written a program remembers the first one that worked — the little jolt of a machine doing precisely what you said. It is the most reliably delightful thing in all of computing, and almost every product built to teach kids to code manages to bury it.

What exists today

Drag the blocks. Complete the puzzle. Collect the badge. The code is a chore standing between the kid and the fun — and the game ends when the curriculum does, because the curriculum was the game. There's no reason to come back on a Saturday.

What we're building

She writes six lines, hits RUN, and watches her bot rumble across a bright world in real time — treads kicking dust, juddering over rock, sliding on ice, chirping happily when it nails the turn she planned. The code isn't the price of the fun. The code is the fun.

The core loop

Six lines. One button. Instant, visible truth.

The gap between writing an instruction and seeing its consequence is a few seconds — and it's legible. She doesn't read an error message. She watches the mistake happen. That is the fastest debugging loop a nine-year-old will ever get, and it's the pedagogical engine of the whole product.

Where the learning hides

The toys aren't decoration. They're the if statements.

Every fun thing in this game is a decision, and every decision is a line of code. She never stops playing in order to learn — the learning is the move.

The toybox

Shots go forward. Drops go behind.

Two different kinds of thinking — aim now versus plan ahead — and a kid feels the difference in the shape of her code. Everything spends from one charge meter, so every action is a trade.

fire("ice")Shot

Freezes their treads solid for a moment. They can't move — but they're never out.

TeachesTiming. Worth nothing unless you follow it up.

fire("paint")Shot

Splatters their lens. radar() starts returning nonsense. Does no damage whatsoever.

TeachesDon't trust your inputs. The only toy that attacks the program.

fire("gust")Shot

A big shove — knocks them a square or two. Ideally straight into a puddle.

TeachesSpatial reasoning. Where they are matters too.

dropSmoke()Drop

Left behind you. Anything chasing through it goes blind. The escape artist's move.

TeachesPlanning ahead. You're setting a trap for a future tick.

dropOil()Drop

A slick behind you. Whoever hits it spins out and loses a turn. The banana peel.

TeachesPrediction. Not where are they — where will they be?

dash(n)Move

Burst 1–3 squares. Escape, close, dodge — and dash into a bot and you shove it clean out of the way.

TeachesArguments have a cost. Over-dash and the pit is your own fault.

Why this becomes a franchise

The engine knows no story.

The fiction never touches the simulator. Art, sound, copy and names live in a swappable pack — so an entirely new world (a rover on Mars, a sub on the seabed, a rig in orbit) is a content drop, not a sequel. Same arenas, same verified grid traces, same pars — completely different game.

Core Invariant
The simulator and the JavaScript API. Never themed — not even a little. A rover on Mars does not get drive(). forward(3) is forward(3) in every skin, because the transferable knowledge is the product. A test greps this layer for story words and fails the build if it finds one.
Workbench Invariant
The editor, the garage, the meters. The tool doesn't change when the world does.
Theme pack Swappable
Art, sound, copy, names. Everything she sees and hears; nothing she reasons about.
The feel layer

The most important sound in a coding game is the one that plays when the code works.

And it should not be a coin. A coin is transactional — it says you scored. But what actually just happened is that a machine understood her, and that is a completely different feeling.

So the bot chirps. It's pleased with her. And when the code is wrong it gives a small, confused boop — never a buzzer, never a red X, never a lost point. It's funny, it never shames, and it makes her want to fix it for the bot. Nintendo has known this for forty years: Mario doesn't get a point sound when he does something great. He says "wa-hoo!" The character reacts.

And every terrain is legible from motion alone, long before she reads a number — the bot judders over rock, labours through mud, and slides right past the square it meant to stop on. She feels the physics before anyone shows her a stat.

Non-negotiable

The safest possible multiplayer game for kids.

Kids compete against each other with zero ability to contact each other. It isn't a feature bolted on — it's a constraint the architecture enforces.

  • No channelThe only thing a kid publishes is a program. No bio, no description, no message, no chat. Nothing to moderate, because there is nothing to say.
  • Nothing diesBots get tagged, dented, towed and rebooted. Never destroyed. It's a law in the spec — and a test enforces the vocabulary.
  • DeterministicSame program, same seed, same result — forever. Nothing can be faked, and every bug is perfectly reproducible.
  • It's real codeShe exports her bot as an actual .js file. What she learned transfers the day she outgrows us.
